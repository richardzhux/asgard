# Chunk 02 (units 821-1720)

Key concepts / sections

- Post-conviction reimbursement for costs of care
- Restitution
- Veterinarian reporting
- Common exemptions to anti-cruelty statutes:
  1. Traditional veterinary practices  
  2. Animals used for medical, educational, or scientific research  
  3. Hunting, fishing, and trapping  
  4. Farmed animals and “standard animal husbandry practices”  
  5. Pest control  

---

Definitions & rules

- Post-conviction reimbursement for costs of care  
  - Many states place a lien on seized animals for the cost of their care if the defendant is convicted.  
  - Practical issue: Many cases settle and many defendants are judgment-proof, so agencies often don’t actually recoup much.

- Restitution  
  - Applies when the defendant does *not* own the animal victim.  
  - Court orders the convicted person to pay the animal’s owner for economic loss, e.g., veterinary bills.  
  - Often implemented via general criminal restitution provisions.

- Veterinarian reporting  
  - As of early 2018:
    - 15 states: mandatory reporting of suspected/known cruelty.  
    - ~20 states: address vet reporting in law/regulation but do *not* mandate.  
  - Vets typically receive some form of immunity from liability when they report.

---

Common exemptions (major way cruelty laws are weakened)

1. Traditional veterinary practices  
   - Governed by state veterinary practice acts.  
   - Typical scope: diagnosis and treatment of disease/injury, administering prescription meds, pregnancy/fertility diagnosis.  
   - “Animal” often defined broadly as any non-human animal (mammals, birds, reptiles, amphibians, fish).  
   - Effect: Broad definition of “veterinary practice” + broad definition of “animal” = wide carve-out from cruelty statutes.
   - Example: Pennsylvania  
     - Cosmetic surgery (e.g., declawing, ear cropping, tail docking) is defined as traditional veterinary practice (63 Pa. Stat. Ann. § 485.3(10)).  
     - Result: those cosmetic surgeries are exempt from the state anti-cruelty law.

2. Animals used for medical, educational, or scientific research  
   - Most states exempt research activities from core anti-cruelty provisions.  
   - Federal baseline: Animal Welfare Act (AWA), 7 U.S.C. §§ 2131–2159  
     - Regulates housing, care, and treatment of certain lab animals in federally funded research.  
     - Does *not* regulate the actual research protocols themselves.  
   - So states typically defer to the federal AWA for lab animals.
   - Example: New Mexico  
     - Federally funded AWA-covered facilities are exempt, *except* when conduct is “knowingly” outside standards in internally approved protocols (N.M. Stat. Ann. § 30-18-1(I)(6)).

3. Hunting, fishing, and trapping  
   - Generally treated as protected/regulated cultural practices.  
   - Anti-cruelty statutes usually explicitly state they do not interfere with:
     - State or federal game laws, and  
     - Other lawful hunting, fishing, and trapping activities.  
   - Additional wildlife protections may come from:
     - Endangered Species Act (ESA), Marine Mammal Protection Act, Migratory Bird Conservation Act, and parallel state laws.  
   - But: most hunted/fished/trapped animals are *not* covered by these specialized protections.  
   - Result: Conduct that would be criminal cruelty if done to a dog or cat may be legal if done to unprotected wildlife or game animals.

4. Farmed animals and routine practices  
   - “Standard animal husbandry practices” generally exempt.  
   - Typical exempt procedures (often performed without anesthesia):
     - Castration  
     - Debeaking  
     - Tail docking  
     - Ear cropping  
     - Dehorning  
   - These exemptions significantly limit anti-cruelty protection for farmed animals.

5. Pest control  
   - Many statutes exempt “commercially acceptable” pest control methods.  
   - Typically covered methods include:
     - Fumigation  
     - Pesticides  
     - Poisoned bait  
     - Traps  
     - Sterilization  

---

Examples mentioned

- Pennsylvania: cosmetic surgeries as “traditional veterinary practice,” thus outside cruelty statute.  
- New Mexico: limited exemption for AWA-covered research facilities only if they comply with internal protocols.

---

Pitfalls / common confusions

- Assuming all harmful practices are covered by cruelty statutes:
  - Many major categories are *explicitly exempt* (vet procedures, research, hunting, farming, pest control).
- Confusing federal vs. state roles:
  - AWA sets minimal treatment standards for lab animals but does *not* regulate research protocols; states frequently just defer to AWA, leaving protocols largely unregulated.
- Overestimating financial remedies:
  - Liens for costs of care and restitution exist on paper, but collection is often ineffective due to settlements and defendants’ inability to pay.  
- Equating protections for companion animals with protections for wildlife or farmed animals:
  - Acts that are clearly criminal for dogs/cats may be lawful for wildlife or livestock due to explicit statutory exemptions or game laws.
