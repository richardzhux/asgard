# Chunk 20 (units 15581-16480)

Key concepts / sections
- Power and problems of judges at dog shows  
- Fraud and “faking” in dog exhibitions and sales  
- Class tensions: “true sportsmen” vs “pure dealers”  
- Founding of the Kennel Club (1873) and the Stud Book (1874)  

Definitions / core ideas
- Judge (in dog shows):  
  Person responsible for assessing dogs’ quality and detecting cheating; public confidence in their honesty was essential for the show system’s legitimacy.

- “Faking” (in dog shows):  
  Artificial alteration of a dog’s appearance to meet breed/show standards, including:
  - Plucking out white hairs  
  - Dyeing the coat  
  - Cutting ear cartilage to make ears droop  
  - Shortening too-long tails  
  - Trimming coat with scissors  

- “Pure dealers”:  
  Disparaging term used by “disinterested” fanciers for people who:
  - Show dogs primarily to win money prizes  
  - Use wins to raise sale value of dogs  
  - Are seen as commercial, unscrupulous, and as hangovers from earlier, lower-class tavern shows.

- “True sportsmen”:  
  Group self-description of fanciers who:
  - “Breed to win” but claim money is “of no moment”  
  - Position themselves as honorable, gentlemanly, and above commercial motives.

- Kennel Club (founded 1873):  
  - Association of “true sportsmen” created to:
    - Combat fraud by formally establishing dogs’ identity and descent  
    - Create a structured, interlocking system of:
      - Registration  
      - Approved shows  
    - Effectively limit competition to a vetted subset of dogs and owners (both canine and human populations are implicitly filtered).

- Stud Book (first volume 1874):  
  - Official Kennel Club register listing pedigreed dogs exhibited since 1859  
  - Central tool for tracking ancestry and legitimacy of show dogs.  

Examples
- Ring behavior by exhibitors:
  - Physically maneuvering dogs to block judge’s view of rivals  
  - Loudly praising their own dogs and criticizing competitors  
  → Described as “good clean fun” even among refined breeders.  

- Behind-the-scenes fraud:
  - Doctored animals; false or misleading catalog descriptions  
  - Buyers paying for stud services of champions and receiving inferior animals instead  
  - Novices sold misrepresented “pedigree” pets  

- Anti-fraud mechanisms for novices:
  - Gordon Stables’ recommendation: use periodicals’ “approval” services:
    - The magazine holds buyer’s money  
    - Only releases it once the animal and pedigree are certified as genuine and acceptable.  

Pitfalls / common confusions
- Not all misbehavior was viewed equally:
  - In-ring gamesmanship (blocking, heckling) was tolerated as playful.  
  - Serious fraud (forging pedigrees, swapping dogs) was condemned as threatening the entire fancy’s respectability.

- “Disinterested” vs actually non-commercial:
  - Self-described “disinterested” dog lovers claimed not to care about money, but:
    - They still “breed to win” and gain prestige;  
    - Their rhetoric helps mark class and moral boundaries against “pure dealers,” not eliminate all self-interest.

- Kennel Club’s role:
  - It was not only about animal standards; it also:
    - Enforced a social boundary, helping make dog showing a respectable, quasi-gentlemanly pursuit and excluding disreputable participants.
