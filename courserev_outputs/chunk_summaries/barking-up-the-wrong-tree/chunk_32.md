Definitions / core ideas
- Breed misidentification:
  - People (including shelter workers, vets, owners) often incorrectly identify dog breeds based on appearance.
  - Certain breeds, especially those perceived as dangerous (e.g., pit bulls), are over-identified as culprits in attacks.
  - This makes any law relying on visual identification or anecdotal reports inherently unreliable and hard to enforce.

- Affect and fear in risk perception:
  - “Affect” = emotional reaction (e.g., fear) that powerfully shapes our judgment of risk.
  - Strong fear leads people to overemphasize and over-identify a perceived danger, regardless of actual risk data.

- Eyewitness unreliability (applied to dogs):
  - Human memory and identification are fragile and easily contaminated by:
    - Expectations and beliefs
    - Desire to help catch a “perpetrator”
    - Passage of time
    - Suggestions from police, co-witnesses, media, etc.
  - Once a memory is contaminated, it cannot be “purified.”
  - These same mechanisms likely distort breed identification in dog incidents.

- Visual risk symbols:
  - Like the mushroom cloud shaping fear of nuclear power, the image of a “charging pit bull with teeth bared” shapes public fear and perceived risk of pit bulls, regardless of actual bite data.

- Breed-Specific Legislation (BSL) and fear:
  - BSL = laws targeting specific dog breeds (e.g., bans or restrictions on pit bulls).
  - The article argues BSL:
    - Is not grounded in solid science.
    - Is not effective at reducing severe or fatal dog bites.
    - Mainly serves to reduce public fear, not actual risk.
  - BSL is a “knee-jerk” response driven by emotional reactions, not rational risk management.

- Behavioral decision theory / prospect theory (setup):
  - Field studying how people actually make decisions under risk (not how they “should”).
  - Key contributors: Tversky, Kahneman, Slovic, Lichtenstein, Fischhoff.
  - Shows humans use shortcuts and are prone to systematic errors when evaluating risks, especially emotionally charged ones (like “vicious dogs”).

Examples mentioned
- Euthanizing dogs “because they have a square head, short hair and a straight tail” (visual stereotype of pit bulls).
- Denver’s ban on any dog that appears to be >50% pit bull.
- Media “hype” and repeated scary images of pit bulls contaminating breed identification and risk judgments.

Pitfalls / common confusions
- Assuming visual identification of dog breed is reliable:
  - In practice, heavily biased and error-prone, especially when fear and media stereotypes are involved.
- Equating public fear with actual risk:
  - BSL may make people feel safer but does little to change real bite risk.
- Treating anecdotal attack stories as solid evidence:
  - Anecdotes are shaped by misidentification, memory contamination, and media framing.
- Believing policy decisions are primarily rational:
  - Research shows emotionally salient risks (like dog attacks) often trigger fast, intuitive, fear-driven responses rather than careful analysis.
