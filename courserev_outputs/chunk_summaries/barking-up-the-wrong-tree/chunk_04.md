  - Scientific data indicate:
    - Dog breed is a poor predictor of aggression.
    - BSL is ineffective at reducing dog bites and wastes government resources.

    - Recommended against BSL.
    - Urged education of humans on dog behavior instead.

  - Communities still rely on old (1987) sound bites and questionable statistics when passing BSL.
  - Media, e.g., Time magazine (2014), continue to recycle decades‑old clichés about pit bulls.
  - Metaphor: “cannot un-ring the BSL bell” – once fear-based policy is entrenched, it is hard to reverse.
  - The problem is as much about human behavior (fear, decision-making) as canine behavior.

Definitions / key terms
- Breed-Specific Legislation (BSL): Laws that restrict or ban particular dog breeds (often pit bulls) on the assumption that those breeds are inherently more dangerous.
- Dog-bite epidemiology: The study of patterns, causes, and effects of dog-bite incidents in populations.
- Paws for Tales: Program allowing children anxious about reading aloud to practice by reading to dogs.

Examples
- Positive pit bull examples countering “vicious” stereotype:
  - Therapy and reading‑program dogs (Leo, Jonny Justice).
  - Gentle companion animals (Sweet Jasmine, Zippy).
- Netherlands study:
  - Concluded that aggression depends on many factors (heredity, experience, socialization, training, health, victim behavior), not just breed.
- Historical/media example:
  - 1987 statistics/sound bites still used; contrasted with cultural markers (Guns N’ Roses album, Sidney Crosby’s birth) to show their age.

Pitfalls / common confusions
- Assuming breed = aggression:
  - Oversimplifies complex behavior influenced by environment, training, and human actions.
- Overreliance on media narratives:
  - Sensational stories and repeated clichés can drive policy more than data.
- Ignoring breed-identification error:
  - BSL presumes accurate identification of breeds, but DNA work shows humans misidentify mixed-breed dogs frequently.
- Confusing fear‑driven policy with evidence‑based policy:
  - Lawmakers may respond to public pressure and vivid incidents rather than overall risk statistics and scientific research.
